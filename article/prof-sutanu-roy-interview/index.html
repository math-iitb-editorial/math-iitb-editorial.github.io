A Journey Through Mathematics and Academia with Prof. Sutanu Roy

Published: 2025-12-20

Dr. Sutanu Roy is an Associate Professor in the Department of Mathematics at IIT Bombay, where he joined the faculty in October 2024 after serving as a faculty member in the School of Mathematical Sciences at the National Institute of Science Education and Research (NISER), Bhubaneswar.

Originally from Kolkata, Dr. Roy completed his undergraduate studies in mathematics at Scottish Church College, University of Calcutta, and went on to earn an M.Sc. in Mathematics and Computing from the Indian Institute of Technology Guwahati. He then pursued doctoral studies in mathematics at the Georg-August-Universität Göttingen, Germany, where he earned his Dr. rer. nat. degree in 2013 under the supervision of Professor Ralf Meyer.

Professor Roy’s research interests centre on quantum groups in the operator-algebraic framework, as well as their interaction with quantum symmetries, which provide a systematic framework for groups and symmetries in the realm of noncommutative geometry.

What initially drew you to mathematics? Was there a specific moment or influence that shaped your interest?

I can’t pinpoint one single moment that “made” me love mathematics. It wasn’t a lightning bolt, it was gradual, something that evolved over almost a decade, through school and my high school years. There was a time when I liked both physics and mathematics, and it was genuinely hard for me to tell which one I was more inclined towards. They seemed to blur into each other. For a long time, I thought I could pursue either of them. However, eventually, during my 12th grade, I decided to pursue a career in mathematics.

If I had to pinpoint one particular incident that significantly changed how I approached maths, it would be in class 4. Until then, I treated mathematics the same way I treated my other subjects. I memorised everything. I didn’t really understand what I was doing; I just remembered procedures. Then, one fine day, our school had an exam filled with conceptual questions. I was utterly unprepared for that kind of paper and performed disastrously. I still remember the embarrassment. But that failure turned out to be a huge eye-opener. I realised that mathematics doesn’t reward memorisation; it rewards understanding. So I consciously changed how I studied. I stopped rote learning and started asking “Why?” instead of just “How?” Within a few months, the change was reflected in my marks, with the next exam going exceptionally well. It was the first time I could see that understanding something deeply was actually more powerful than merely memorising it mechanically. That small incident made a big difference. I began to enjoy mathematics for its logic, its clarity, and its sense of cause and effect. And, of course, I owe a great deal to my teachers, one of my uncles, and my parents, who constantly encouraged me. So, it wasn’t a one-day decision. It was a long process shaped by many people and experiences, all of which led me toward mathematics.

Did you always envision yourself in academia, or were there alternate paths you considered?

Honestly, I never imagined doing anything outside academia. Not seriously, at least. From the time I was a student, my goals were always simple and short-term, almost naïve, in retrospect. When I was in college, I wanted to be a teacher at a school. During my master’s program, I aspired to teach at a college. And when I was pursuing my PhD, my dream was to join a university as a faculty member. My goals evolved naturally with each stage of my academic journey, but they never strayed outside the classroom or the realm of academia.

There was a brief moment during my postdoctoral research in Canada, near the end of my post-doctoral fellowship, and before my first job in India, when I began thinking about the industry. But it was not a serious plan. It was more of an idle curiosity, a thought like, “Maybe I should explore what’s out there.” It lasted a few weeks, maybe months, but deep down, I knew I wouldn’t actually leave academia. So, in that sense, yes, academia was always the plan. I never had an alternative path laid out, which is not the most practical approach, I’ll admit. But that was how I functioned.

Is there a reason you never considered anything outside academia?

Partly, it’s because I never had that exposure. Growing up, nobody around me worked in the corporate or industrial side of things, so I didn’t have much of a sense of what that life would look like. But more importantly, I was, and still am, deeply committed to the idea of mathematics as a lifelong pursuit. One of my teachers had once said something that really stayed with me: “If you decide to pursue a career in mathematics, then even if you end up in a humble position, you shouldn’t regret that choice.” The idea was that your satisfaction should come from the work itself, not from where it takes you. It’s not always a practical way to think, but I somehow took it to heart. So even if, in the worst-case scenario, I hadn’t landed an academic job, I think I’d still have stayed in the same world, maybe teaching informally. I had a very extreme, almost idealistic view of the profession. For better or worse, I’ve always followed that line of thought.

What keeps you motivated to pursue research and teaching after so many years?

For me, teaching is a source of energy. Every time I teach a course, even if it’s one I’ve taught multiple times, I prepare from scratch. I don’t recycle old notes because each time, I’m a slightly different person with a bit more experience and perspective. That difference changes how I see the subject. When I revisit a theorem after a few years, I often find that it offers a new perspective. Perhaps a proof now feels simpler, or maybe I’ve noticed a subtlety I overlooked before. I also like exploring different textbooks. Each author has their own way of presenting an idea, and that variety keeps things fresh. Sometimes I find a new proof in a book and think, “Oh, this is such a beautiful way to see it.” Those small discoveries keep me excited.

Interacting with students is another joy. Although students nowadays are sometimes less vocal in class, the few who ask questions often prompt me to rethink how I explain things. And occasionally, I learn something from them: new perspectives and new ways to communicate or to simplify an idea.

As for research, it’s slower but equally fulfilling. You rarely achieve significant results quickly; sometimes, even proving a lemma can take weeks. It’s a long, continuous process. The key is to continually learn new techniques and stay up-to-date with developments in your field. For me, teaching and research complement each other beautifully. When I feel stuck in research, I dive into teaching. When I make progress in research, I ride that momentum. It’s a self-sustaining cycle with one fuelling the other.

What does a day in the life of a researcher actually look like?

That depends a lot on the person. Some people have a very regimented schedule: they start at nine, take coffee breaks, read for specific hours, go jogging at five, and so on. I’ve never been able to work that way. In my experience, research is far more unpredictable. You might spend an entire day reading one paper and realise by evening that it doesn’t help you. Or you could sit down casually one morning and suddenly find an argument that keeps you up all night. During my PhD, my days had this uneven rhythm. There were periods of intense energy followed by stretches of frustration. You start out excited, thinking your problem is new and untouched, only to discover that people have been aware of parts of it for decades. That realisation can be disheartening. But it’s also part of the process and teaches you humility.

Having a good doctoral mentor helps tremendously. They’ve already experienced those emotional swings and can tell you whether your approach is likely to be effective. Sometimes, just hearing “Yes, this is a good direction” can save you months of doubt. In theoretical research, you don't get tangible results every day. It's a virtual experiment with unseen progress. Most of the time, your output is invisible; it lives in the structure of your thoughts, not on a screen or graph. But when a proof finally clicks, when you write the last line of something that’s been haunting you for months, that’s one of the best feelings in the world. It may only last for a day or two, but it’s enough to keep you going.

Can you recall a breakthrough moment that reaffirmed your decision to stay in academia?

Yes, vividly. It was during the final year of my PhD in Göttingen, just before Christmas. My supervisor had told me that I could start writing my thesis, which was reassuring, but there was one problem I had been unable to solve for over a year. Then Christmas came, and Göttingen emptied out. It’s a small university town, and during the holidays, everything shuts down. My building, which usually housed around fifty inhabitants, had only two or three of us left. It was eerily quiet, almost haunting, but also peaceful. One fine night, with no distractions, I sat down to work on that problem again. Over the course of three days, I managed to close the gaps, fill in all the missing arguments, and prove the result.

That short burst of progress became one of the chapters of my thesis. What made it special was not just the result but the timing. I had been on the verge of losing confidence in myself, wondering if I was cut out for research at all. Solving it reminded me why I was here. Ironically, within a few weeks, the excitement faded, and I began to see the result as “obvious”. That’s how research works. Your big victories shrink in hindsight.

How do you see the balance between collaboration and individuality in research, and how has collaboration worked for you?

I’ve been fortunate to have very positive experiences with collaboration. For me, a good collaboration begins with mutual respect and complementary strengths. The best projects I’ve worked on have been with people who knew something I didn’t and vice versa. It’s not about dividing a problem mechanically, such as “you do part A, I’ll do part B”. It’s about bringing two different sets of tools to the same goal. Sometimes, my collaborator was faster, sometimes I was. What mattered was trust, because if either of us lost trust in the other during one of their slow patches, the whole collaborative effort would fall to its knees.

How should a student passionate about mathematics choose between academia and industry?

That’s a profoundly personal decision, and no one else can make it for you. I don’t think professors or mentors should dictate it either; they can advise, not decide.

Firstly, you have to be honest with yourself. Research is not a short-term commitment; it can easily shape the next ten years of your life. You need to ask yourself, “How good am I at handling uncertainty? Can I work through frustration without visible rewards?” Because in research, failure is routine, and success is slow. The other reality is that in academia, you spend a lot of time thinking about things that very few people around you understand. So your frustrations are often hard to share. Your family may sympathise, but they won’t grasp what’s wrong. You learn to live with that solitude. On the other hand, if you find joy in the process itself and if the act of thinking, learning, and inching forward feels rewarding, then research is worth it. There’s no absolute right or wrong path. The important thing is to decide knowingly, not by default.

It is better to talk to someone who is already in industry to help decide whether you'll find yourself to a better fit there.

What are the biggest misconceptions students usually have about research in mathematics?

One big misconception is that mathematics is uniquely difficult, and that not being good at it is somehow “normal”. People even say it proudly that “I’m terrible at maths.” You wouldn’t usually hear that about other subjects, but with maths, people sometimes boast about it.

Among students who do want to pursue maths, the common misconception is about what progress looks like. In mathematics, progress is rarely visible. You can’t see it the way you see a structure taking shape on a construction site. In research, you’re also building something - an idea or a proof - but it lives entirely in your head until it’s complete. Because of that, many students fail to recognise small successes. They think only in terms of final theorems, not the gradual learning that leads there. But research requires you to notice and celebrate the quieter wins: understanding a tough paper, finding a new way to restate a lemma, or discovering a counterexample that clarifies your path. The output in mathematics is often invisible but deeply meaningful if you learn to see it.

What advice would you give to students who feel intimidated or fear they might not be good enough for research?

Firstly, the phrase “good enough” is misleading. In a course, your ability is judged by a snapshot: one day, one paper, one problem set. Research doesn’t work that way. It’s about persistence and self-direction. When you start a PhD, treat it as your job. Your supervisor is there to guide you, not to do the thinking for you. You’ll have long stretches where no one is telling you what to do, and that’s part of the training. It teaches independence.

The transition from coursework to research is like shifting from learning recipes to inventing your own dishes. You know the ingredients, but you have to decide what to make with them. It can be daunting, but if you keep asking questions, reading, discussing, and thinking on your own, you slowly get the rhythm of it. Also, avoid constantly comparing yourself to others, as research is a nonlinear process. Some people solve big problems in a year, some take five. Consistency and curiosity is what drives one through research!

What qualities are essential for someone considering a career in pure mathematics?

You must enjoy solving problems; that’s the heart of it. Every textbook you read gives you a handful of exercises; don’t skip them. Try to solve at least a few. Until you’ve struggled through problems on your own, your understanding remains abstract. When you solve problems, push them further: drop a hypothesis, change an assumption, see what fails. Sometimes, that process leads you to completely new questions. It’s how many good research ideas begin.

Another essential quality is curiosity, along with a constant urge to ask questions. Don’t hesitate to ask “stupid” questions; they’re often the ones that reveal the deepest gaps in understanding. I wish I’d realised that sooner. In pure maths, proposing a question can be as valuable as answering one. You often discover that to answer a question properly, you need to invent new definitions or frameworks. Finding the right definition is an art; sometimes it’s the backbone of a research article.

Looking back, is there something you wish you had known as a student or early-career academic?

Plenty. For one, I wish I had asked more questions in class. I used to be shy about speaking up, afraid of asking something trivial. That was a mistake. The classroom is the safest place to be wrong.

I also wish I had studied more broadly during my PhD, rather than narrowing down too early. I should have attended more seminars and workshops. Your PhD is the last period in life when you can devote almost all your time to learning. After that, jobs, grants, and responsibilities take over. My supervisor encouraged me to explore other topics, and I didn’t listen as much as I should have.

And finally, I wish I had realised how much you can gain by simply paying attention in class. If you stay fully engaged during lectures, you save hours later. I was fortunate to have had wonderful teachers at every stage, from school to college to my PhD. If I could redo those years, I’d make the most of every single lecture. As a faculty member, I now understand how challenging it is to find time to learn something new from scratch. The patience and energy you have as a student are the best resources you’ll ever have. Use it well.
