Pit Stop Analysis in Formula 1 Racing

Published: 2025-10-28

“You don’t win a race on a pit stop, but you lose a race by having a bad pit stop.”

Rich Wolverson (Head of Race Team Operations, Red Bull Racing)

In Formula 1, a race where fans marvel at overtakes and top speeds exceeding 300 km/h, seasoned followers know that the most decisive seconds often occur when the car is stationary. They know that races are often won and lost not only on the track but in the pit lane. The pit stop, a choreographed act of human precision and mechanical coordination, has evolved from a routine necessity into a critical element of race strategy and team performance.

What exactly happens in a pit stop, and how do they influence the race outcomes? What conclusions can we get by analysing years of pit stop statistics? Let us unwrap the strategies behind one of F1’s underrated arts.

What Happens in a Pit Stop?

At its core, a Formula 1 pit stop involves changing all four tyres, sometimes adjusting the front wing, and sending the car back out onto the track - all in a matter of seconds. Each mechanic has a designated role during this blink-of-an-eye time period.

The average pit stop for a top team takes about 2 to 2.5 seconds, but the fastest on record (by McLaren Racing Limited pit crew during lap 27 at the Qatar GP in 2023) clocked a mind-blowing 1.80 seconds. Two blinks and the car is back on track!

While such stops appear effortless, every millisecond is perfectly engineered. Teams analyse slow-motion footage, track hand movements, and optimise coordination using motion-capture and AI-based feedback systems. This pursuit of perfection has turned the pit stop into a technological and strategic weapon.

The Evolution of Pit Stop Speeds

Historical data shows a clear trend toward shorter and more consistent pit stops over the past decade. In the early 2010s, the average stop duration hovered around 3.5-4 seconds. By 2019-2020, elite teams like Red Bull and Mercedes were routinely performing pit stops under 2.5 seconds.

However, 2021 brought a turning point. The FIA introduced safety regulations requiring teams to confirm wheel nut attachment electronically before releasing cars, which slightly slowed average times. Data from 2021-2023 show averages creeping back toward the 2.7–3.0 second range, but with fewer unsafe releases - a necessary trade-off between speed and safety.

Undercut and Overcut: Strategy vs. Speed

Pit stops are not merely a mechanical necessity; there is also an artistic strategy behind them. The timing of these stops also defines the outcome. The two dominant strategies prevalent among pit lane tactics are the undercut and the overcut.

The Undercut

Let us consider Drivers A and B. Driver A is trailing Driver B by 1.8 seconds before the pit window. Driver A dives into the pits first, incurring a pit-lane loss of 22.0s. After leaving the pit, the fresh-tyre advantage gives A roughly 1.5s per lap for the next two laps. Later, if Driver B pits with a 23.0s stop, Driver A has gained a total of 3.0s on the track and comparatively gained 1.0s against Driver B in the pit, giving it a gain of 4 seconds. This puts it comfortably ahead of Driver B when B comes out of the pit.

Undercut used the fresh-tyre advantage to push ahead in the race. An example of the usage of this was during the Azerbaijan GP, where George Russel’s undercut on Carlos Sainz helped George to recover from 5th to get a second place on the podium.

The Overcut

Now, let us revisit Drivers A and B; this time, Driver A is ahead of Driver B by 1.5 seconds before the pit window opens. Now Driver B decides to pit first in order to attempt an undercut. His pit-lane loss is 22.0 seconds. But after rejoining, his tyres are still cold, which costs him 1.5 seconds on his out-lap while they warm up. Driver A stays over longer for two more laps on warm tyres, losing only 0.3 seconds per lap to degradation. This means over two laps, Driver A lost 0.6 seconds while Driver B lost 1.5 seconds. Now Driver A pits, costing him 22.0 seconds, but when he rejoins, he is still 0.9 seconds ahead, additionally enough to come out first.

Overcuts utilise the comparative advantage of warm tyres, causing less degradation when optimally timed to get a time advantage over the rival’s cold-tyre phase of their early pitstop. Recent examples of this include Lando Norris’s overcut on Charles Leclerc during the 2024 Miami GP, where Norris stayed out longer, utilised his warm tyres to set faster laps, and gained track position as Leclerc’s tyres took time to heat up, ultimately helping Norris go on to win the race.

Statistical Insights from Pit Stop Data

Constructor-Level Differences

Teams like Red Bull, Mercedes, and Ferrari consistently show lower median pit stop times with minimal variance. Their boxplots cluster tightly around the 2.2–2.5 second mark, demonstrating not just speed but repeatability.
In contrast, smaller teams, such as Williams, Sauber/Alfa Romeo, and Haas F1, exhibit a greater spread, suggesting more frequent operational inconsistencies.

Pit Stop Duration vs. Finising Position

The correlation between pit stop time and race finishing position reveals the tangible impact of pit-lane efficiency. When plotting pit stop duration against finishing position, a downward trend emerges: shorter pit stops are statistically associated with better race finishes.

Most podium finishes occur with pit stop durations of under 25 seconds, totalling less than 25 seconds of pit-lane time (including entry and exit). Meanwhile, stops exceeding 40 seconds correspond with finishing outside the top 10 or even retirement.

Human Factors and Consistency

Speed is one part of the equation; consistency is another. The best pit crews demonstrate minimal variability, with each stop executed to a consistent, almost-perfect standard. Red Bull’s data shows a standard deviation under 0.15 seconds across dozens of races, whereas midfield teams may fluctuate by 0.4-0.6 seconds. Across 23-race seasons, such small numbers add up enough to swing an entire race outcome.

Reaction times, ergonomics, muscle fatigue, and even breathing patterns are monitored, with pit-crew being trained like athletes.

Technology, AI, and the Future of Pit Stop Strategy

A new era of pit stop analysis has dawned, with the use of predictive modelling and automation on a much larger scale than seen before. Modern teams utilise machine learning algorithms to simulate pit windows in real-time. These models utilise various metrics, such as tyre degradation, track temperature, and rival lap times, to predict the optimal lap for a pit stop or undercut attempt.

Computer vision systems also analyse thousands of pit stop videos, identifying micro-delays such as slow wheel-gun attachment or driver misalignment. AI-driven motion tracking can pinpoint inefficiencies invisible to the human eye.

With the introduction of semi-automated wheel guns and standardised pit equipment in future regulations, teams may soon approach the human limit of pit stop speed.

The Hidden Battle Behind Every Race

The spectacle of Formula 1 may unfold on the circuit, but one of its fiercest battles occurs behind the white lines of the pit lane. Whether through lightning-fast stops or perfectly timed undercuts, pit-stop analytics prove that in Formula 1, every millisecond counts.
